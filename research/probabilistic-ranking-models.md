# Probabilistic Ranking Models 

**Bradley–Terry (BT) Model:**  Each item \(i\) has a latent score \(\beta_i\), and the probability \(P(i\!>\!j)\) of \(i\) beating \(j\) is modeled by a logistic function, e.g. 
\[
P(i>j) = \frac{e^{\beta_i}}{e^{\beta_i} + e^{\beta_j}}.
\] 
Bradley and Terry (1952) formalized this model (building on earlier work by Zermelo 1929).  In practice one fits the \(\{\beta_i\}\) by maximum likelihood (Zermelo’s method) or MAP.  The likelihood is concave and has a unique maximum (up to an additive constant) under mild conditions.  Because the model is only identified up to an overall shift, one must impose a constraint (e.g. \(\sum_i \beta_i=0\) or set one \(\beta\) to 0).  In sparse or degenerate data (e.g. an item wins every match), the MLE can diverge (“complete separation”).  In that case one can regularize the likelihood or use a prior: for example, Firth’s bias-reduced logistic fit (equivalent to a Jeffreys prior) yields finite estimates even under complete separation.  Equivalently, Bayesian BT typically places a weak normal prior \(\beta_i\sim N(0,\sigma^2)\), which softly pins the scores to a mean of zero and avoids infinite estimates.

**Thurstone–Mosteller (Case V):**  An alternative to BT is Thurstone’s law of comparative judgment (Case V) from psychometrics, in which each item \(i\) has a latent utility \(s_i\) and the difference \(s_i-s_j\) is corrupted by Gaussian noise.  Then 
\[
P(i>j)=\Phi\!\Bigl(\frac{s_i - s_j}{\sqrt{2}\,\sigma}\Bigr),
\] 
where \(\Phi\) is the standard normal CDF.  This “probit” model is mathematically analogous to BT, differing only in link function (Gaussian vs logistic).  Under full data both models yield similar rankings, but BT (logit) is more common in large-scale settings.  

**Plackett–Luce (PL) Model:**  The PL model generalizes BT to full or partial rankings.  It assigns each item \(i\) a positive strength \(p_i\) (often \(p_i=\exp(\beta_i)\)), and the probability of a complete ranking \(y_1\succ y_2\succ \cdots \succ y_N\) is 
\[
P(y_1\succ \cdots\succ y_N) = \prod_{k=1}^N \frac{p_{y_k}}{\sum_{m=k}^N p_{y_m}},
\] 
i.e. a sequential draw without replacement (Luce’s choice axiom).  For pairwise data this reduces to BT.  PL was introduced by Luce (1959) and Plackett (1975) as a natural “choice by elimination” model.  One can also treat PL via latent variables (e.g. i.i.d. exponentials) to enable Bayesian inference.  (A related model is the Mallows model, which assumes a fixed “true” ranking with random flips; Mallows models can handle non-transitivity but are often harder to fit.)

**Inference and Identifiability:**  In all these models the log-likelihood is concave in the \(\beta\)’s (with appropriate constraints) so standard solvers apply (iterative scaling, MM or Newton).  Uniqueness holds up to the usual gauge constraint.  Incomplete comparison graphs (some missing pairs) cause no fundamental problem as long as the comparison graph is connected.  However, with very sparse data or one-sided wins, the ML solution may not exist.  Regularization/priors are essential in that case.  For example, the R package *BradleyTerry2* (Turner & Firth, 2012+) implements bias-reduced MLE: it adds a penalty (Firth’s method) so that “all estimated coefficients…are finite, even in complete separation”.  In Bayesian terms, a small normal or Dirichlet prior on strengths ensures identifiability and avoids infinities.  (BradleyTerry2 can also fit BT via probit or cauchit links, handle ties [Davidson 1970], covariates, etc., but basic BT with logistic link is most common.) 

# Bayesian Inference for Static Scores 

A Bayesian approach places a prior on each item’s score and computes a posterior given the pairwise data.  This yields a full posterior distribution (not just point estimates) and allows credible intervals or uncertainty-aware decisions.  For example, Mattos & Ramos (2022) describe the *bpcs* package for Bayesian BT: they use normal priors \(\beta_i\sim N(0,\sigma^2)\) to “softly” identify the model, which yields finite posterior even when ML fails. The posterior is intractable analytically (non-conjugate logistic), but can be approximated by Laplace or other methods.  A **Laplace (Gaussian) approximation** takes the MAP (or MLE) and fits a multivariate normal using the Hessian of the log-posterior.  This is equivalent to doing a Gaussian approximation to the likelihood (related to Fisher information).  In practice Laplace often works well for abundant data, but can underestimate uncertainty when data are few or multimodal.  (Christiano et al.’s ML-from-preferences notes that Laplace *underestimates* posterior variance if the MAP is near a boundary or data are limited.) 

To get better uncertainty, one can use **expectation propagation (EP)** or **MCMC**.  For instance, the Python library *choix* implements EP to approximate the BT posterior (and more general Luce models).  Full MCMC (e.g. via Stan) will sample the posterior of the \(\beta\)'s, giving exact credible intervals at greater cost.  In either case, one obtains a covariance or variance for each item’s score, enabling uncertainty quantification.  Gaussian-process models extend this: Chu & Ghahramani (2005) introduced a **Gaussian-Process Preference Learning** approach, putting a GP prior on a latent utility function \(f(i)\) over items (or item features).  The likelihood is still that \(P(i>j)=\Phi((f(i)-f(j))/\sqrt{2})\) (probit), but now \(f\sim\mathcal{GP}\).  This captures correlations among items if features are available, and yields posterior mean and variance of \(f(i)\) for each item.  Learning is done via approximate methods (Laplace or EP on the GP).  Thus one can maintain per-item posterior uncertainty even in a static-score model (no time evolution) by using a Gaussian posterior or a GP posterior. 

**Summary:**  In summary, static Bayesian models for pairwise data give a posterior over scores \(\{\beta_i\}\).  Implementation options include Laplace or EP approximations (cheap, e.g. choix’s EP for Bradley–Terry), or Gibbs/Metropolis MCMC (e.g. bpcs/Stan).  GP-based methods (Chu & Ghahramani, 2005) provide a nonparametric alternative with full Bayesian uncertainty.  In all cases, one can extract means and variances for each item’s score to guide decision-making. 

# Active Pair Selection 

To minimize queries, one selects each next pair *adaptively*.  A principled way is to use Bayesian experimental design.  For example, Guo et al. (2018) formulate several objectives for choosing a batch \(S\) of pairs: 
- **Mutual Information (Maxent):** pick \(S\) to maximize \(I(\beta;Y_S)\), the expected information gain about scores. 
- **Expected Entropy Reduction:** minimize the expected label entropy \(H(Y_S|\beta=\hat\beta)\) at the current MAP estimate. 
- **D-optimal (Log-Det):** maximize \(\log\det(F)\) of the Fisher information matrix (equivalently, minimize the posterior volume). 
- **A-optimal (Trace):** minimize \(\mathrm{tr}(F^{-1})\) (total variance) via Fisher information. 

These criteria all favor queries that are maximally informative.  In practice, computing them exactly is expensive for \(O(N^2)\) candidate pairs.  Guo *et al.* note that mutual information is submodular but analytically intractable (requires approximating a non-Gaussian posterior).  Greedy approximations or batch algorithms can be used, but they require repeated posterior updates and determinant computations (costly when \(N\sim100\)).  

An alternative is **uncertainty sampling** or **Thompson sampling**.  In a fully Bayesian approach, one might sample a score vector from the current posterior and then choose the pair with maximal disagreement (e.g. closest scores or highest entropy).  In the dueling-bandits literature, **Thompson Sampling** algorithms (e.g. Double-Thompson for Condorcet or Borda winners) have been proposed for active preference (Wu & Liu 2016) and are implemented in toolkits.  Thompson methods implicitly balance exploration/exploitation by randomizing over the posterior.  

Surprisingly simple heuristics also work well.  For instance, Maystre & Grossglauser (2017) show that *repeated sorting* is an effective strategy.  They propose repeatedly applying a sorting algorithm (e.g. QuickSort) to adaptively choose pairs.  Theoretically, if comparisons were noise-free, any sort would recover the ranking in \(O(N\log N)\) comparisons (vs \(\Omega(N^2)\) for random sampling).  They prove that under a Bradley–Terry noise model, the outputs of a noisy Quicksort still approximate the true ranking (displacement error is small).  Empirically, their *Just-Sort-It* strategy matches or exceeds more complex active methods in accuracy while using very few comparisons.  Key advantages: it requires no model training at each step, it is \(O(N\log N)\) total cost, and no tuning parameters. 

**Tradeoffs:**  In summary, *exact* Bayesian design (MI, D-opt) is statistically optimal but can be computationally heavy for \(N\sim100\).  Heuristics like uncertainty sampling or sorting are fast but may be suboptimal in theory.  Dueling-bandit algorithms (Thompson/UCB variants) offer a compromise with low per-step cost (sampling a few pairs) and theoretical regret guarantees.  For most practical single-user settings, repeated sorting or picking the pair with closest current score estimates (highest uncertainty) has been shown to give excellent accuracy for few queries. 

# Noise, Inconsistency, and Robustness 

Real comparisons are noisy and possibly intransitive.  **Sample-complexity bounds:**  Information-theoretic analyses of “noisy sorting” show that \(\Omega(N\log N)\) comparisons are required to exactly recover the true ranking in the worst case under noise.  Ren *et al.* (NeurIPS 2019) derive lower bounds on queries for exact ranking that imply an \(O(N\log N)\) term (depending on score gaps).  Thus one cannot do much better than \(N\log N\) even adaptively, under mild assumptions.  

**Explicit error models:**  One can incorporate noise by adding an error parameter to BT.  For example, Crowdsourcing BT variants (like *Crowd-BT* by Chen *et al.* 2013) introduce a per-annotator “flip” probability \(\eta_k\): each comparison outcome is correct with prob.\(\eta_k\).  Crowd-BT learns both item scores and annotator reliabilities, effectively down-weighting spammers.  In a single-user setting one could likewise assume the user has error rate \(\eta\), adjusting likelihood \(P(i>j)=\eta\,\sigma(\beta_i-\beta_j)+ (1-\eta)(1-\sigma(\beta_i-\beta_j))\).  Such models tolerate occasional flips in pair outcomes. 

**Intransitivity (HodgeRank):**  When preferences exhibit cycles (A>B, B>C, C>A), BT/PL can only fit a “closest” consistent ranking.  A recent approach (HodgeRank, Jiang *et al.* 2011) decomposes the comparison graph via discrete Hodge theory.  It finds a global score vector \(\{s_i\}\) (gradient flow) that best fits the data in least-squares sense, and quantifies the cyclic (curl) component that cannot be explained by any ranking.  In practice, HodgeRank yields the nearest consistent ranking and a measure of inconsistency (sum of squared cycles).  This is not a probabilistic model, but provides insight into robustness: one can filter out noisy/cyclic edges or inflate uncertainties on inconsistent comparisons. 

In summary, robustness can come from theory (knowing \(\Omega(N\log N)\) is needed), explicit modeling of noise (e.g. annotator flip like Crowd-BT or Mallows-type models), or spectral methods (HodgeRank) to detect cycles.  For a single relatively reliable user, occasional mistakes can be handled by a slight “slack” in the BT likelihood or a bias term, and by choosing informative comparisons to average out errors.

# Practical Recommendations and Software 

For a one-time ranking task with \(N\approx 20–300\) items and on the order of a few hundred pairwise queries, a **static Bradley–Terry (or Plackett–Luce)** model with an active strategy is recommended.  Concretely: fit a BT model (e.g. log-scores) by MAP or Bayesian inference with a weak prior, and repeatedly select queries to maximally reduce uncertainty.  A **Bayesian approach** is advisable to obtain uncertainties; for example, one could use a Gaussian prior on log-strengths and run a Laplace or EP inference at each step (the *choix* library does this efficiently).  

For active selection, the simplest effective method is *repeated sorting*: repeatedly shuffle and quicksort the current estimate of items, asking each comparison once.  This yields near \(O(N\log N)\) queries and is extremely fast (see Maystre & Grossglauser 2017).  Alternatively, at each step pick the pair with closest current scores (highest entropy).  If one prefers principled Bayesian design and can afford more computation, using a greedy information criterion (e.g. choose the pair that maximizes expected Fisher information gain) can help refine the last few uncertain comparisons.  

**Accuracy per question:** Empirical studies (Maystre *et al.*) find that active policies (sort, uncertainty) greatly outperform random pairing.  In practice, one often achieves a useful ranking (low Kendall-\(\tau\) error) with only \(O(N\log N)\) comparisons.  For \(N\) up to a few hundred and say 200–500 comparisons, these methods typically give a near-final ranking.  

**Software:** Several libraries implement these ideas.  In Python, the *choix* package by Lucas Maystre provides BT/PL inference with EM/MM and an EP-based approximate Bayesian solver.  For experiment design, the *dueling-bandit* toolkit (Bhandari et al.) implements Thompson-sampling and UCB variants for pairwise feedback.  In R, the **BradleyTerry2** package (Turner & Firth, J.Stat.Soft. 2012) fits BT models (logit, probit) and even performs bias-reduction.  The *bpcs* package (Mattos & Ramos, 2022) offers Bayesian BT via Stan, which can automatically handle sparsity and yield credible intervals.  

**Summary:**  A practical pipeline is: use Bradley–Terry with a mild normal prior, fit (e.g. by MAP or EP) after each batch, and select the next pair by a simple rule (e.g. quicksort or highest posterior variance).  This combines the statistical consistency of BT/PL models with the efficiency of adaptive querying.  This approach has been validated in practice and enjoys reference implementations (choix, BradleyTerry2, dueling-bandit toolkits) for ease of use. 

**References:**  Foundational sources include Bradley & Terry (1952), Thurstone (1927), and Plackett (1975); Bayesian BT treatments (e.g. Caron & Doucet 2012 JMLR), GP preference learning (Chu & Ghahramani 2005), and experimental design for BT (Guo *et al.* 2018) all directly inform the above strategies. For handling noise, see Chen *et al.* (WSDM 2013) on Crowd-BT and Jiang *et al.* (NIPS 2011) on HodgeRank. Practical codebases include *choix* and *dueling-bandit*.
